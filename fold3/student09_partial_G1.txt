
At the lowest level, transistors act as tiny switches that form logic gates for arithmetic and control. These gates are assembled into a datapath governed by a clock so the CPU can fetch instructions, decode them, execute operations, and write results. Memory hierarchy mediates speed and scale through registers, caches, main memory, and persistent storage.

Building on that core, the operating system schedules work, manages virtual memory, and brokers I/O through drivers. Compilers translate high-level programs into machine instructions, and runtimes provide services such as dynamic loading. Networks extend the computer by enabling remote communication over layered protocols. Modern systems also emphasize energy efficiency and concurrency: multicore processors parallelize workloads, while power management units gate unused circuits. Security boundaries in hardware and software—page tables, process isolation, and cryptography—protect data as computation proceeds. The computer thus integrates deterministic primitives with coordination mechanisms to perform complex tasks reliably while balancing performance, efficiency, and safety.
