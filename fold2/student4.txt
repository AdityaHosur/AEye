Many students now treat social media as a study companion, shaping how they find, trade, and retain information. Used with intention, feeds can spark peer explanations, quick feedback, and bite-sized microlearning that fits into short breaks. Tags and curated threads often surface strong resources faster than generic search. Yet the same systems are optimized for attention rather than understanding: infinite scroll fractures focus and nudges shallow processing over deliberate practice. Pings and pop-ups splinter study blocks, degrading working memory and the ability to connect ideas. Recommendation loops create echo chambers that amplify popular, partial viewpoints while hiding dissenting evidence.

Effective learners establish boundaries: silence nonessential alerts, batch messages, and move notes into tools that enable spaced repetition and active recall. They turn short posts into structured outlines, examples, and practice prompts. Group chats work best when they track sources and decisions, not hot takes. Visual summaries can help, but only when paired with self-testing instead of passive consumption. Ethics count too: credit creators, avoid spreading misinformation, and protect privacy when screenshots include classmates.
